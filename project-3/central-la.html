Central / South Central LA

Central and South Central Los Angeles is a dense urban core encompassing neighborhoods such as Downtown LA, Boyle Heights, South LA, and Watts. It is historically underserved in terms of green infrastructure and public investment, facing high urban heat exposure, air pollution, and concentrated traffic corridors. Structural inequities continue to shape heat vulnerability and environmental stress in this region.

Overview — Central / South Central LA

Average Annual Temperature: ~74°F
Extreme Heat Days: 40–50 days/year above 95°F
Heat Island Index: +7–10°F warmer than coastal LA

Population: ~1.8 million residents
Racial/Ethnic Composition:
• ~60% Latinx
• ~25% Black
• ~10% White
• ~5% Asian / Other

Heat Vulnerability Indicators:
• High rates of cardiovascular and respiratory conditions
• Concentrated low-income housing and older adult populations
• Elevated exposure near highways and industrial areas

Heat Inequity Indicators

Tree Canopy Coverage: ~8–12% (County avg ~21%)
Lowest canopy areas: South LA, Watts, Boyle Heights

Median Household Income: ~$50,000 (some neighborhoods ~35k)
Poverty Rate: 20–35% depending on neighborhood

Cooling Centers: ~20 locations in central and south LA
Homes Without AC: ~25–35%
Utility Cost Burden: 8–12% in lower-income areas

Transportation Exposure:
• Walkability scores 45–55
• Bus and metro riders often exposed to heat on long waits
• Freeway-adjacent neighborhoods experience elevated heat

Historical Trends — Central / South Central LA

Temperature Change:
• +3.5–4.5°F average rise since 1950
• Extreme heat days more than doubled since 1980
• Nighttime warming intensified by dense urban development

Urban Development:
• Rapid postwar industrial and residential growth
• Freeway construction increased impervious surfaces
• Reduction of open land and public green space

Demographic Change:
• Growth of Latinx and Black populations after 1980
• Displacement and gentrification in central areas
• Ongoing economic and racial segregation patterns

Environmental Justice History:
• Industrial facilities concentrated in low-income neighborhoods
• Minimal public investment in cooling, shade, and green infrastructure

Solutions & Interventions

Green Infrastructure:
• Tree planting initiatives and community greening projects
• Cool pavement and reflective surface pilots
• Shade structures along busy streets and transit stops

Community Solutions:
• Neighborhood cooling hubs and youth climate programs
• Local advocacy for equitable heat protections
• Community-led environmental justice projects

Government Policies:
• Region included in LA County Heat Action Plan
• Targeted investments in “shade deserts”
• Incentives for reflective roofs and heat-resistant materials

Tech & Design Interventions:
• Smart heat sensors in schools and recreation centers
• Reflective roofing, insulation improvements, and cool materials
• Heat-resistant building standards for new developments
